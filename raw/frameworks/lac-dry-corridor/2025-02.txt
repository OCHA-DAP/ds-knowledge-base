El Salvador, Guatemala and Honduras
2024-25
Updated in February 2025

1. Overview
Summary
The purpose of this document is to present the framework for anticipatory action (AA) for drought in Guatemala, Honduras
and El Salvador (Dry Corridor), including the forecasting trigger (the model), the pre-agreed action plans (the delivery) and
the pre-arranged financing (the money).
This document should be read in conjunction with the Anticipatory Action Concept Note for the Dry Corridor.

Executive summary
This document presents the conceptual framework for collective and pilot anticipatory action in the Dry Corridor. It includes
pre-agreed arrangements for forecasts and triggers, an action plan and budgeting. This framework is meant to be a guiding
umbrella framework to support countries in developing their country-specific anticipatory action drought frameworks in
Central America. Adjustments at country level may include for example national forecast sources, the trigger windows
according to local agricultural seasonality, and the selection of context-specific anticipatory actions.
El Nino phenomenon is one of the most serious threats facing the Dry Corridor, with significant impacts on the economy
and people's livelihoods. The concept note for this framework outlines plans to implement anticipatory action ahead of an
expected drought. This choice is explained, on the one hand, by the considerable number of people whose living conditions
will be affected and, on the other, by the availability of proven drought prediction tools.
The aim of this framework is to minimize humanitarian needs in an anticipatory manner through a package of multi-sectoral
activities for people at risk of drought impacts in 3 countries likely to be affected. As soon as the triggering threshold is
reached (in one or more communes), activity packages will be implemented to meet the needs of the population at risk.
Crisis timeline: Climatically, the dry corridor experiences periods of rain and drought that are associated with seasonal
increases in humanitarian needs. For example, the rainy season coincides with the lean season, and therefore with
humanitarian needs, as food stocks are almost exhausted from the previous harvest. For this AA Framework, the partners
established a crisis chronology to answer the questions: How does a severe drought impact the intensity and timing of
humanitarian needs within the Dry corridor? What actions are required and when to minimize the needs?
The trigger model adopted for decision-making in this framework was developed in partnership with FAO (Food and
Agriculture Organization), WFP (World Food Programme), UNICEF (United Nations Children’s Fund), Pan American Health
Organization (PAHO/WHO) and the OCHA (Office for the Coordination of Humanitarian Affairs) Center for Humanitarian
Data (CHD). The framework considers two trigger "windows" for packages of anticipatory interventions.
OCHA's CHD will monitor seasonal forecasts data. UN Agencies will lead the targeting with a set timeframe and criteria.
Financing. CERF (Central Emergency Response Fund) has pre-arranged up to 10.5 million USD for the implementation of
drought anticipatory actions in the Dry Corridor. Based on the regional framework approved by the Emergency Relief
Coordinator with annexes for each country, CERF will make the disbursement once the trigger threshold has been reached.
Receipt of CERF funding for anticipatory action does not preclude (or guarantee) additional CERF funding for a
complementary rapid response to save lives resulting from the same (or other) shocks.
Anticipatory actions. UN agencies have identified anticipatory actions in the following sectors: Food security (food and
livelihoods/agriculture), Nutrition, WASH and Health. These actions are detailed below. All activities should start once the
triggering threshold has been reached. Cross-cutting protection and accountability aspects have also been considered.

2. Introduction
Objectives of anticipatory action within the Dry Corridor
The aim of this anticipatory action framework is to reduce the humanitarian impact of drought on people at risk in the Dry
Corridor through a multi-sectoral package of projects that unfold as soon as the trigger threshold is reached.
In other words, once a severe drought is forecasted, UN Agencies and their partners, including governments, will reach
vulnerable people identified within the dry corridor’s most vulnerable areas to drought before the humanitarian impact of the
shock through a multi-sectoral intervention package.
This anticipatory action package therefore methodically combines "three plus one" components agreed in advance,
comprising 3 core elements, all of which are underpinned by a clear learning, monitoring and evaluation plan:
●

A robust forecasting embedded in a clear decision-making process (the model).

●

Pre-agreed action plans that can fundamentally alter the trajectory of the crisis (the delivery).

●

Pre-arranged finance (the money).

3. Forecast
Drought in the dry corridor
Particularly vulnerable to climate change are the Dry Corridor areas of Guatemala, El Salvador and Honduras. Since 2001,
these areas have suffered from irregular drought patterns due to changes in the El Niño-Southern Oscillation (ENSO).
Drought impact has been especially severe in Honduras and Guatemala.
El Niño typically leads to below-average rainfall over Central America. An El Niño event aggravates dry conditions by
bringing poor spatial and temporal distribution of rains during a critical stage of crop development, often causing a late onset
to the rains and/or increased dry spells, an early cessation threatening an already fragile agricultural sector marked by low
resilience and inadequate preparedness to handle weather shocks. Areas at highest risk go across the Dry Corridor
stretching across Guatemala, Honduras, and El Salvador, including also Atlantic-facing regions and northern Guatemala.
El Nino-related drought typically intensifies and increases the needs for humanitarian assistance in the region. Potential
impacts of concern include rising food prices, increase in malnutrition – in a region where, despite low prevalence, an
increase in cases of acute malnutrition has been reported recently - and heightened protection concerns, particularly for
vulnerable groups 1.

In context of drought, the overall nutritional intake decreases, and poor households are at higher risk to suffer multiple
micronutrient deficits, contributing to higher prevalence of underweight and stunting among children under five (especially
for reduced ability to access food, particularly animal-sourced foods high in caloric energy, protein, and fat).
During droughts, access to water is reduced both in terms of quantity and quality - at home, in schools and in healthcare-facilities – increasing the risk of spreading respiratory diseases, and acute diarrheal diseases (such as cholera,
typhoid) and other water-borne diseases, especially in children living in unplanned urban dwellings. Remaining productive
water points are more vulnerable to contamination and are used by a growing number of people, and the risk of overabstraction can foster tensions between users. The increase of diseases is reinforced by poor sanitation and hygiene
practices, compounded by the proliferation of vector-borne diseases in unsafe environments (including zika, yellow fever,
dengue, etc.).
Gender inequities intensify during droughts. Water scarcity affects menstrual hygiene in young girls and women, while
children (often responsible for collecting the water) spend less time at school, with an enormous burden aggravated by
higher exposure to safety risks and exploitation while fetching water. During the last prolonged drought recorded in
Honduras (2016-2020), one in four people reported an increase in violence during the emergency, including an increased
risk for children and an even more pronounced risk for girls (37%) than boys (17%). Moreover, poor nutrition can impact
the school performance of students, their motivation to attend school, and acquisition of knowledge and skills necessary
to advance. The disruption of education and the lack of a safe and protective environment for children amplifies their risk
of exposure to violence and other risks, especially those whose families have been fragmented due to internal displacement
or migration.
Droughts also affect poverty, violence, and migration. The poorest households in areas of the Honduran, El Salvadoran,
Guatemalan Dry Corridor of the Western Altiplano and Alta Verapaz,are considered the most at-risk since they have seen
their livelihoods deteriorate and have been forced to employ unsustainable coping strategies until the arrival of the first
harvest in September. El Niño is expected to further affect the capability of vulnerable households to access quality
services, including indigenous groups.
Poorest households suffer a significant narrowing of income generation options, which leads to high-risk activities that
can lead to progressive draining of economic assets and stocks and increasing inequality and marginalization for the most
vulnerable. Children are most at risk of being left with limited or no access to the essential services and may be forced into
child labor to compensate for loss of agriculture and livelihoods. This change in livelihoods can produce an increase of
insecurity, especially for women and children, with increased risk of being exposed to internal or international migration,
exploitation and abuse, including sexually, as extreme survival strategies, with further limitation of children attending
schools, due to the households needs for economic support and an increased risk of child labor and exploitation.

4. Anticipatory Action Plan
Selection criteria
Anticipatory actions will aim to interrupt the pathways outlined in the crisis timeline section above by targeting populations
most at risk of being impacted by a shock. The following criteria were applied to select actions:
1. Anticipatory character: Is the action effective in preventing or reducing the humanitarian impact of the shock?
2. Ability to deliver within the window of opportunity: Is it possible to carry out the action effectively with the
available forecast lead time 2, i.e. in the window of opportunity?
3. Operational capacity: Do the agency and its implementing partners have the institutional capacity (thematic,
logistic, administrative, financial, human resources) to implement the action effectively given the lead time and
scale?
4. No regrets approach: In the case of a false alarm, will the proposed actions benefit, rather than harm, the targeted
population?
In principle, the activities under window A and window B form two collective packages. Activities under one window will be
activated together, targeting the same populations. Some activities are particularly time-critical and have minimum lead
times (defined as the time between initiation of the project and the beginning of delivery of assistance to the population).
Activities whose lead time does not permit timely delivery of assistance if activation occurs in a later monitoring month, shall
be labelled as such in the framework and CERF projects.
CERF cannot fund activities that are no longer feasible at the moment of triggering, according to the lead times provided in
the country-specific annexes. In case of ‘late activation’ (i.e. after the latest feasible time, as per Agencies’ provided
information), project budgets will be reduced accordingly.

Priority interventions
The pre-agreed actions will focus on the following core objectives:
●

Advising small-holder farmers on appropriate decisions to take before and during drought, based on agroclimatic
information

●

Protecting food production, productive assets and access to food, through inputs distribution, water harvesting,
cash injections and good agricultural practices [Food Security – FAO & WFP]

●

Mitigating the risk of acute malnutrition in children under 5 years of age, ensuring early identification and referral to
life-saving treatment (reinforce surveillance, prepositioning of supplies, mobilization to ensure community-based
access the services) [Nutrition - Unicef]

●

Protecting the access to safe water and hygiene practices, including in health facilities and schools (water sources
protection/rehabilitation, water storage supplies distribution, chemicals distribution for water quality, awareness
raising on water and hygiene management) [WASH – Unicef]

●

Preventing acute malnutrition, epidemic outbreaks and health care associated infections through comprehensive
nutritional surveillance, control and containment of epidemic outbreaks of infectious and communicable diseases
[Health – WHO]

●

Contain epidemic outbreaks of infectious and communicable diseases by strengthening of monitoring systems,
collection, storage and supply of water in health and sanitary facilities [Health – WHO]

It is worth noting that the three countries will follow parallel, yet separate activation and monitoring streams. Activation points
differ from country to country, so do weather forecasts. Hence, one country could potentially reach activation point in a
given month, and another country in the following month (or, not reaching it at all). The result is, possibly, a scattered
activation.

Monitoring of Triggers
For transparency and accountability, CHD will keep informed concerned stakeholders (OCHA Country and Regional Offices,
Agencies Country and Regional Offices, OCHA NY [CERF, OAD, HFSA]) throughout the monitoring periods/windows,
regardless of the achievements of trigger-point or not. Upon receipt of an alert from CHD on possible activation, a meeting
will be convened at country level, under the leadership of the RC/HC, to discuss the results.
In the unlikely event that CHD communicates the reaching of trigger-point for a country, but in-country stakeholders disagree
(opting for a ‘non-activation’), the RC/HC will notify agency focal points and OCHA NY (CERF, OAD, HFSA) about the
decision to not activate the framework.
It is important to note that, in case of non-activation, other funding mechanisms are available from CERF, including the
Rapid Response for Early Action. Hence, a no-activation does not preclude the mobilization of resources from other
mechanisms.

Targeting
Each country defines its targeting based on a model that encompasses a geographical scope for identifying areas most
affected by drought, and a people’s approach that identifies the most vulnerable people within those areas. This is described
in the respective country annex.
Implementing agencies will proactively seek synergies and complementary actions to assist the same communities, and
possibly the same households, with most of the services included in the present framework. The rationale will be to provide
comprehensive and complementary anticipatory assistance to families and communities, with the objective of mitigating the
drought's effect.
By having the same communities (or families) assisted with most, if not all, the thematic areas included in the framework
(food security, nutrition, water, sanitation, hygiene and health), it is expected that this holistic delivery will result in synergetic
outcomes amongst the thematic areas and obtaining multiplier effects.

Ensuring readiness for a timely implementation
UN Agencies have established presence at regional, subregional and country base levels, including sound relationships
with ministries of agriculture, national Disaster Risk Management (DRM) systems and other relevant national and
subregional institutions. As well, FAO and WFP have Anticipatory Action Plans for Drought in Honduras, Guatemala and El
Salvador, including funding from ECHO.

5. Pre-agreed Financing
Pre-arranged finance to fuel anticipatory action is critical to enable timely, effective anticipatory action. A key
recommendation from the Emergency Relief Coordinator is to find co-financing for coordinated anticipatory action in the
Dry Corridor to ensure available funding is mutually reenforcing. Of course, partner agencies invest considerable resources
in building coordinated anticipatory action, but also for preparedness already.

CERF
For the anticipatory action framework in the Dry Corridor, the ERC (Emergency Relief Coordinator) agreed to pre-arrange
up to $10.5 million from CERF for two years (2024-2025). Funding will be disbursed, if the above-outlined triggers are
reached.
All funds will be disbursed through CERF’s Rapid Response window; CERF has not created a separate “Anticipatory Action”
window.
CERF will disburse funds once three conditions are met:
●

Endorsement by the relevant Resident/Humanitarian Coordinator of the country-specific Anticipatory Action
validation protocol and the CERF application package comprising agency-specific project proposals and agencyspecific budgets.

●

Endorsement by the Emergency Relief Coordinator of the country-specific Anticipatory Action framework and the
CERF application package comprising agency-specific project proposals and agency-specific budgets (this can be
a pre-agreed endorsement).

●

Activation of the pre-agreed trigger threshold, within a maximum of two years from the ERC’s endorsement of the
framework.

Other donors
This framework for anticipatory action is not limited to CERF-funded activities. There are various instruments, including the
World Bank’s Crisis Response Window (CRW), Education Cannot Wait (ECW), Start Network, bilateral donors and UN
agencies’ internal reserve funds that can, within their own established criteria and in complementarity, finance part of this
plan when the need arises.

6. Learning
Monitoring
Post-distribution monitoring (PDM) will be done by UN agencies under the AA framework, in coordination with implementing
partners and relevant stakeholders, to measure results achievement.
Each agency will use its existing monitoring and evaluation systems to collect and track data on implementation progress
and results achieved. This can be financed by CERF funds as usual. All results will be shared.
To ensure robust evidence with causal attribution to AA, each implementing agency is encouraged, wherever possible to
use a comparative research design in their M&E plan to estimate what would have happened without anticipated actions
(the "counterfactual"). In practical terms, this means that in addition to collecting outcome indicator data from AA

beneficiaries, outcome data should also be collected from a comparison group made up of individuals/households who
share the same socio-economic characteristics and are also affected by drought as the intervention group receiving AA.
Examples of relevant comparison groups could be individuals/households who receive "no assistance", "no AA but only a
post-shock response", or who receive different configurations of the planned AA package, for example by varying the
transfer amount/reduction/complementary actions. It is up to each implementing agency to determine whether this kind of
study is feasible and practical in its operational context.
At a minimum, and to enable maximum learning, each agency's M&E will need to integrate and report on a set of common
questions, including:
●
●

What was the impact of anticipatory activities on the survival or quality of life of beneficiaries?
Was the assistance provided in time, ahead of the crisis, in line with the anticipatory nature of the framework? Did the
timing make a difference?

●

Can we draw a comparison between anticipatory activities in the framework, and past response interventions?

●

Can we draw a comparison between communities receiving anticipatory activities, and those not receiving?

●

Did activities result in any multiplier or spillover effects?

Reports will be shared with relevant stakeholders, including actions taken to resolve identified implementation issues.
To capitalize on respective technical expertise, and avoid unnecessary, multiple visits to the same communities and
households, Agencies will agree on ways to coordinate M&E efforts. Ideally, a comprehensive M&E framework will be
agreed at country-level, amongst participating Agencies, including a common form for data capturing, timing,
responsibilities. OCHA can facilitate these discussions, and compile learnings from the various sources in one
comprehensive document.

Independent evaluation
If possible, the anticipatory action framework will be subject to a final independent evaluation to assess its implementation
and the added value of the anticipatory activities, to generate lessons for anticipatory action and improve future operations.
All evaluations will be carried out with the technical support of the sub-regional, regional and global teams of the agencies
involved in the process, and in coordination with implementing partners.
