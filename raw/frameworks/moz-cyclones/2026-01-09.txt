Mozambique - Cyclones
As of 9 January 2026

1. Executive Summary
This document presents the framework for anticipatory action (AA) in Mozambique, including the trigger, the pre-agreed
action plan and the pre-arranged financing.
The context: Mozambique is regularly impacted by cyclones, especially in the low-lying coastal areas that are home to a
majority of the population. Since 2017 alone, some 5 million people have been affected, mostly in the provinces of
Zambezia, Nampula and Sofala. Moreover, cyclones are the natural hazard with the greatest attributable economic losses.
Since 2000, Mozambique has been impacted by a major tropical cyclone every other year on average. The cyclones that
hit the Mozambican coast are increasing in number and intensity1.
The objective: This framework seeks to provide coordinated anticipatory action to people at risk of or affected by cyclones
in Mozambique. The initiative provides life-saving assistance to people across all coastal provinces: Cabo Delgado,
Nampula, Zambezia, Sofala, Inhambane, Gaza, Maputo and Maputo City.
The development process: The framework was developed through a participatory process led by the Resident and
Humanitarian Coordinator and coordinated by OCHA Mozambique in collaboration with the government-led national
technical working group on anticipatory action.
The trigger
The financing and activities included in this framework will be triggered by either the following readiness trigger, action
trigger or observational trigger.
•

•

•

Readiness trigger (lead time up to 120 hours before landfall, monitored by OCHA/CHD):
o The readiness trigger is using the same conditions defined for the action trigger (see below), but is
considering forecasts with lead times between 96 and 120 hours
Action trigger (lead time between 96 and 6 hours before landfall, monitored by INAM):
o The exposed area corresponding to a tropical cyclone of at least 119km/h overlaps with at least one of the
coastal provinces: Cabo Delgado, Nampula, Zambezia, Sofala, Inhambane, Gaza, Maputo and Maputo
City
Observational trigger (last forecast before or at landfall up to 48 hours after landfall, monitored by OCHA/CHD):
o Forecasts issued around landfall indicate that landfall will be within the selected provinces and the intensity
at landfall will be at least a tropical cyclone of 119km/h OR
o The sum of rainfall within a given province the days before, of and after landfall (sum of rainfall over 3 days)
around the landfall location exceeds 120 mm.
o If the observational rainfall trigger is reached only, either the HC or the CERF secretariat can stop an
activation within 48 hours of landfall, should they determine that available information indicates that the
humanitarian impact is not severe to an extent that justifies a CERF allocation.

Partners also agreed to conform with the government-mandated cut-off time of 6 hours before landfall for the action trigger
to protect humanitarian personnel and people targeted. The observational trigger for windspeed is assessed around the
time of landfall and may lead to the activation of the framework. If a trigger is reached at any time during the 6 hours before
expected landfall, no anticipatory activities will begin or be carried out during this window.

The action plan
The framework will enable agencies to implement anticipatory activities in a coordinated manner. The pre-agreed actions
will focus on the following core objectives:
•

•
•
•

Communicating risks, engaging with communities, and early warning messaging: Partners will collaborate
to disseminate joint early warning signals as well as sector-specific messaging, including on people’s rights to safe
and free assistance. All partners will harmonize their messages through national government platforms in addition
to agency-specific channels.
Supporting people in temporary accommodation centers (TACs): Partners will ensure TACs are prepared, and
people receive food, water, sanitation and hygiene (WASH) and other assistance.
Providing in-kind assistance: A set of in-kind assistance activities such as the provision of shelter kits, dignity
kits and hygiene and water treatment products.
Facilitating access to services: The framework supports access to health, protection, (including gender-based
violence (GBV)), and WASH services.

Activities were designed to be applicable for both anticipatory action and early response in case of an observational trigger
being met.
The financing
The Emergency Relief Coordinator (ERC) has pre-arranged up to $4.5 million from the Central Emergency Response Fund
(CERF) to support the present framework. The funding will allow partners to provide assistance in the Food Security
($1.3M), Shelter ($819k), WASH ($750kM), Health ($450k), Protection ($300k), Sexual and Reproductive Health ($300k),
GBV ($300k), CCCM ($231k), and Logistics ($50k) sectors and areas of responsibilities. In addition, agencies will provide
funding through their internal mechanisms to increase the scale of the assistance provided.
The learning
The present framework has incorporated several lessons learned from previous activations. In case of an activation, an
after-action review will be conducted to gather further lessons learned from the implementation of the framework. Partners
also commit to engage in more comprehensive learning efforts in the future.

2. Introduction
Objectives of anticipatory action
There is broad agreement for the international humanitarian sector to scale up anticipatory action. An anticipatory approach
leads to a more effective, efficient and dignified response. It also protects hard-won development gains.
Today, we can predict with increasing confidence the occurrence and humanitarian impact of certain climate-related shocks
and infectious disease outbreaks. By combining different analytical approaches, out-of-the-ordinary weather events can not
only be predicted, but their projected impact can proactively be mitigated based on pre-identified anticipatory actions.
Building on growing evidence that acting prior to the onset of a predictable hazard enables more (cost-)effective
humanitarian response, OCHA facilitates anticipatory action globally, including through the setup of Anticipatory Action
frameworks.
Each framework comprises 3 core elements, all of which are underpinned by a clear learning, monitoring and evaluation
plan:
•
•
•

Robust forecasting embedded in a clear decision-making process (the trigger).
Pre-agreed activities that can fundamentally alter the trajectory of the crisis (the action plan).
Pre-arranged funding (the financing).

In addition to the 3 core elements, OCHA and partners invest in documenting evidence and learning from each framework
to inform the improvement and scale-up of anticipatory action.
Each anticipatory action framework is tailored to the local context. Additional core principles that apply to the framework in
Mozambique can be found in the annex.

Exposure to and humanitarian impact of cyclones in Mozambique
Mozambique is regularly impacted by cyclones, especially in the low-lying coastal areas that are home to a majority of the
population. Since 2017, some 5 million people have been affected, mostly in the provinces of Zambezia, Nampula and
Sofala. Moreover, cyclones are the natural hazard with the greatest attributable economic losses. For instance, tropical

Cyclone Idai, which hit Mozambique in 2019, was one of the two most costly events (estimated loss of US$ 1.96 billion) in
Africa over the past 50 years. The annual cyclone season in Mozambique usually lasts from November until March.
However, events may also occur outside of this period. Historical data from the National Institute for Disaster Management
and Risk Reduction (Instituto Nacional de Gestão e Redução do Risco de Desastres, INGD) shows that the impact of
cyclones in Mozambique has varied, affecting between 73,000 people (Cyclone Chalane in 2020) and 1.5 million people
(Cyclone Idai in 2019). All cyclones have led to increased protection needs including loss of civil documentation, heightened
vulnerability of persons with specific needs including elderly people and persons with disabilities, family separation, and
increased gender-based violence (GBV) risks. Women and girls have been among the most vulnerable groups during and
after the disasters. They face increased risks of GBV, displacement, and loss of livelihoods. Women, particularly in rural
areas, are also heavily affected due to challenges in accessing relief and recovery resources. In 2023, Tropical Cyclone
Freddy hit Mozambique twice, causing destructive winds, extreme rainfall, and widespread flooding. Despite accurate early
warnings and coordinated disaster management, significant damage occurred, affecting infrastructure, displacing
populations, and disrupting essential services. Roads were cut off, hindering social services, trade and provision of
healthcare thus worsening the cholera outbreak that had started in September 2022. During the 2024/2025 cyclone season,
three consecutive tropical systems, Chido, Dikeledi and Jude, made landfall between December and March, collectively
affecting 1.3 million people across multiple provinces in Mozambique but particularly Cabo Delgado and Nampula. Cyclone
Chido caused extensive damage in conflict-affected districts in Cabo Delgado, including Ancuabe, Chiure, Mecúfi, Metuge,
and Pemba, compounding displacement and protection concerns. Heavy rains linked to Cyclones Dikeledi and Jude
triggered flash floods in Sofala, Zambézia and Manica that destroyed roads, bridges and schools, and isolated communities
for days. The cyclones that hit the Mozambican coast are increasing in number and intensity2. Between 2000 and 2022,
Mozambique has been impacted by a major tropical cyclone every other year on average (see figure below).

3. Coordination arrangements
Core group
This framework was developed under the leadership of the Resident and Humanitarian Coordinator by a core
group comprised of IOM, UNFPA, UNHCR, UNICEF, and WFP. Relevant coordination fora, such as the Humanitarian
Country Team (HCT) and the Inter-Cluster Coordination Group (ICCG) were consulted. The group closely collaborated with
the wider National Technical Working Group on Anticipatory Action.

National Technical Working Group on Anticipatory Action
The National Technical Working Group on Anticipatory Action (NTWG-AA) in Mozambique is a multisectoral coordination
body led by the National Institute for Disaster Risk Management and Reduction (INGD) that brings together government
agencies, UN organizations, international and national NGOs, Red Cross/Red Crescent partners, donors and academia to
coordinate anticipatory action across multiple hazards. The working group is structured into three sub-groups: one focused
on forecasting and early warning to define risk thresholds, another providing guidance on selecting impactful interventions
for anticipatory action protocols, and a third dedicated to coordinating financing mechanisms. Under government leadership,
the TWG has successfully coordinated activations during the 2024/25 cyclone season and represents a leading example
of institutionalizing anticipatory action within national disaster risk management systems. Collective lessons collated by the
NTWG-AA from the 2024/25 cyclone season AA activation have informed the revisions in this framework.

Synergies with other initiatives
Under the leadership of the Government, this framework is aligned with and complements the efforts of other anticipatory
action initiatives for cyclones, including those by WFP and the Mozambique Red Cross. Details can be found in Table 1.
Moreover, the framework contributes to the Government’s vision of reducing the vulnerability of its population to predictable
shocks through a multi-hazard approach. It thus complements AA initiatives for drought, floods and cholera led by the
Government and supported by WFP, FAO, WHO, UNICEF, the Mozambique Red Cross, Save the Children, and others.

4. Forecast and Trigger
Forecasts
Under the World Meteorological Organization’s Tropical Cyclone Programme, the Regional Specialized Meteorological
Centre (RSMC) in La Réunion has the responsibility for detecting and forecasting tropical cyclones (TCs) in its zone of
responsibility, which includes the South-West Indian Ocean basin, encompassing Mozambique. As such, RSMC La
Réunion’s TC forecasts are the internationally recognized authoritative source for TCs in this region. RSMC La Réunion is
operated by Météo-France.
RSMC La Réunion issues official TC track forecasts with a 120-hour lead time. These forecasts include the TC’s forecasted
category and position and 5-day TC formation risk outlooks. This framework includes an observational component that uses
the forecasts issued around landfall and rainfall data from NASA’s Integrated Multi-satellitE Retrievals for GPM (IMERG)4
on the days before, of and after landfall.

observational trigger. The activation thresholds and data sources have been selected to ensure full integration with the
government-led AA activities and to capture most of the historical rapid response allocations made by CERF while
maintaining a return period for activation of around 1-in-3 years. The framework relies on the following triggers to release
pre-arranged financing and implement activities.

Roles and responsibilities in monitoring the trigger
The monitoring of the action triggers is done using PRISM6, INAM’s system developed in close collaboration with WFP,
which sends out a notification to a pre-determined mailing list that includes INAM, WFP and OCHA.
During the monitoring period, every 6 hours, the forecast released by RSMC in La Réunion is evaluated to determine
whether the trigger has been reached. The process is outlined below:
•
•

The forecast, containing up to 120-hour lead time, is produced by the RSMC Centre.
It is automatically added to an FTP server7 managed by the RSMC. The next steps are then carried out
automatically using PRISM. From the FTP server:
o Forecast files are automatically downloaded and processed
o The forecast is analyzed for wind speeds against the threshold where they overlap with predefined
geographic zones.
o Data is validated against thresholds for tropical cyclones (at least 119 km/h) for the 8 selected provinces..
▪ If a trigger threshold is met, the system automatically sends an email to INAM, WFP’s and OCHA’s
monitoring team to notify them that the threshold has been reached.
▪ OCHA Centre for Humanitarian Data/ OCHA Mozambique emails the relevant parties under the
activation protocol (draft activation messages can be found in the Annex)
▪ If no trigger is reached, the system monitors the next forecast cycle.

While the action trigger will be monitored using the tools and analyses provided by INAM, the readiness as well as
observational windspeed and rainfall triggers, specific to this framework, will be monitored by the Data Science team at the
UN OCHA Centre for Humanitarian Data.
Monitoring will take place from 1 October to 30 April. If seasonal forecasts indicate a significant risk of events outside of
these months, the monitoring period may be extended.

Activation protocol
Pre-defining this activation protocol is critical in order to avoid delays, miscommunication, uncertainty or errors once a
trigger threshold is reached. Activation of the action triggers will be formally decided by the government’s Technical Council
on Disaster Management (Conselho Técnico de Gestão e Redução do Risco de Desastres, CTGD). The readiness and
observational triggers, which are not currently included in the national AA manual, will be monitored and communicated by
OCHA. Additionally, informal communication channels will be maintained to ensure effective information sharing.

5. Anticipatory Action Plan
Selection criteria
Anticipatory actions aim to protect populations most at risk of being impacted by a shock. The following criteria were applied
to select actions:
1. Anticipatory character: Is the action effective in preventing or reducing the humanitarian impact of the shock?
2. Ability to deliver within the window of opportunity: Is it possible to carry out the action effectively with the
available forecast lead time, i.e. in the window of opportunity between the trigger and the cyclone making landfall?
3. Operational capacity: Do the agency and its implementing partners have the demonstrated institutional capacity
(thematic, logistic, administrative, financial, human resources) to implement the action effectively given the lead
time and scale?
4. No regrets approach: In the case of a false alarm, will the proposed actions benefit rather than negatively affect
the targeted population?
Activities were designed to be applicable for both anticipatory action and early response. Complete implementation of early
response activities – in the case of an observational trigger – should not exceed 2 weeks after landfall.

Priority interventions
The pre-agreed actions, identified in collaboration with the national technical working group on anticipatory action, will
focus on the following core objectives:
•

•
•

Communicating risks, engaging with communities, and early warning messaging: Partners will collaborate
to disseminate joint early warning signals as well as sector-specific messaging, including on people’s rights to safe
and free assistance. All partners will harmonize their messages through national government platforms in addition
to agency-specific channels.
Supporting people in temporary accommodation centers (TACs): Partners will ensure TACs are prepared, and
people receive food, WASH and other assistance.
Providing in-kind assistance: A set of in-kind assistance activities such as the provision of shelter kits, dignity

Facilitating access to services: The framework supports access to health, protection, (including gender-based

Climate adaptation
In addition to their life-saving nature, many of the activities in this framework have been selected due to their ability to
strengthen communities’ resilience to the effects of climate change. Examples include: the dissemination of early warning
messages; promotion of safe hygiene and sanitation practices; protection of WASH infrastructures in public institutions and
communities; strengthening and protection of public infrastructures and roads (e.g. quick drainage cleaning, leveling and
sand bags in critical points, improving road accessibility); distribution of water treatment products; cash and in-kind
assistance for shelter reinforcement; reinforcing preparedness of the Local Disaster Risk Committees and health facility
support.

Geographical prioritization
In line with the national standard operating procedures, the framework will be implemented in the following provinces
covering the entire coast of Mozambique: Cabo Delgado, Nampula, Zambezia, Sofala, Inhambane, Gaza, Maputo and
Maputo City (see map below). The RC/HC may decide to expand the geographical scope of early response activities
based on the observed impact, including cyclone-induced flooding. All partners have confirmed their operational response
capacity10.

Targeting
Partners will jointly target temporary accommodation centers identified in coordination with the government. Agencies will
collaborate to identify households, including women-headed households, households with vulnerable older people, and
households with a people with disabilities receiving joint assistance as a preparedness activity. After cyclone landfall,
partners will coordinate their activities, including targeting and identification and referral of people with protection risks, with

Beneficiary identification and registration
While a significant portion of the in-kind assistance and services will be provided in temporary accommodation centers, the
only way of ensuring that sector-specific cash and certain in-kind assistance can be distributed to those in need in the very
short window of opportunity prior to cyclone landfall is by pre-registering households and individuals in advance.
Registration processes must be inclusive and accessible to women and girls and other vulnerable groups, including people
with disabilities and older people and those in rural or hard-to-reach areas.
IOM
While the Government conducts registration, IOM through displacement tracking matrix (DTM) and camp coordination and
camp management (CCCM) provides technical assistance, tools, and resources to enhance efficiency, inclusiveness and
coverage.
UNFPA
While working closely with its national partners, as well as with the government, UNFPA will identify and register vulnerable
women and girls in high-risk areas who might require support during or after a cyclone. Pre-crisis vulnerability data, including
information on pregnant women, and displaced people, will also be utilized. Additionally, GBV Safety Audits will be
conducted to further assess risks and identify vulnerable women and girls. Besides, the Mobile sexual and reproductive
health (SRH) Team will conduct real-time registration of target groups in need of SRH support.
UNHCR
UNHCR conducts rapid protection assessments to identify those at heightened protection risk and assess immediate needs,
risks, and gaps within affected communities. A key protection component of the project is the identification and referral of
persons with specific needs—including people with disabilities, older persons, unaccompanied children, and GBV
survivors—to appropriate services. Mobile protection desks provide basic psychological support, raise awareness of
available services, and ensure inclusive feedback and complaint mechanisms for all, especially vulnerable groups.
UNICEF
UNICEF’s services are a mix of community and household level beneficiaries. For community services, for example,
deployment of mobile health teams, or provision of health kits to the district authorities, no beneficiary registration is required
ahead of time. The Ministry of Health records user data so UNICEF can understand the number of users, visit purposes,
etc. Similarly, water trucking, water and sanitation in institutions and water infrastructure do not require beneficiary
registration; services are available for those who need them. For distribution of health kits and WASH hygiene kits, this will
be prioritized for those staying in accommodation centers. Registration will be coordinated with the government and center
officials. Lastly, messaging will be done through community radios, mobile teams or other means and do not require
registration.
WFP
The successful implementation of WFP’s anticipatory action activities does not require pre-registration of beneficiaries since
there is no provision of sector-specific cash or commodities outside of temporary accommodation centers. Early warning
information will be disseminated for all populations in high-risk areas, while food assistance will be provided to populations
evacuated to temporary accommodation centers.
For the case of early response where populations outside of TACs might be assisted, targeting and registration of
beneficiaries will be conducted in coordination with the government and other humanitarian organizations.

Ensuring preparedness for a timely implementation
Anticipatory action requires a significant level of preparedness to be able to respond quickly and effectively before a shock
occurs and during its early stages. This is especially true for sudden-onset shocks. As a result, partners have made
investments to enhance their operational readiness and be able to implement their activities without delay.
IOM
Displacement Tracking Matrix (DTM)
DTM enables decision makers and responders to provide populations with better context specific assistance. As part of this
framework, IOM will pre-deploy planning, pre-position data collection tools, and deploy enumerators as early response.
Deployment planning includes pre-identifying enumerators and data collection teams for immediate deployment and
establishing clear communication and reporting structures with the government, internally, and among partners to enable
rapid data flow and analysis. Pre-positioning efforts involve updating data collection tools tailored to anticipated disaster
impacts, providing these tools and resources to enumerators and partners in targeted areas, and offering training to
enumerators and government counterparts. During early response, enumerators are deployed to affected areas to collect
preliminary data on population counts and urgent needs. This data is promptly shared with the government, partners, and
sectors to inform early response decision-making.
Camp Coordination and Camp Management (CCCM)
CCCM ensures effective coordination and support through information dissemination, deployment of teams, site/
accommodation center improvement, and early response activities. Information, education, and communication (IEC)
materials are produced and distributed ahead of cyclone landfall, and early warning messages are disseminated through
established communication channels, including community focal points, local authorities, megaphones, and public address
systems, alongside the establishment of information points to keep communities informed. CCCM teams are deployed,
prioritizing experienced field staff, to conduct site visits and joint assessments with government authorities, including
assessments of accommodation and evacuation centers, provide referrals, assist affected populations, and support
authorities with registration and documentation. Communities are equipped with pre-positioned site improvement tools for
preparedness and initial response activities, such as cleaning and organizing. Cash-based interventions, including cashfor-work programs, are implemented to support affected populations, particularly for site/ accommodation center/ road
cleaning and improvement activities. In the early response phase, CCCM teams maintain an on-site presence to assess
needs, facilitate multi-sectoral responses, and strengthen Accountability to Affected Populations (AAP) and PSEA. Referral
and complaints/feedback mechanisms are also established to enhance responsiveness and community engagement,
ensuring activities are implemented immediately once early warning and response triggers are reached through close
coordination with government counterparts.
Shelter
The Shelter/NFI component ensures targeted, needs-based assistance through rapid household assessments and
coordination with Cluster partners to inform and deduplicate the distribution of Non-Food Items (NFIs). Beneficiary eligibility
will be validated in close collaboration with local authorities and communities, with particular attention to vulnerable groups,
including older persons, people with disabilities, and female-headed households. Implementation will be closely coordinated
with the Shelter Cluster, which leads needs assessments, gap analysis, and pipeline stock management, to prevent
duplication and ensure complementarity with other partners.
As part of readiness effort, Shelter/NFI procured and Pre-positioned NFIs, such as tarpaulins and shelter repair kits, will be
mobilized to high-risk districts. Accessible distribution points will be established to ensure equitable access. Following
action trigger, NFIs will be distributed to households, accompanied by awareness sessions and demonstrations on
the correct use of items to maximize effectiveness and durability of assistance.
UNFPA
To ensure rapid action within trigger thresholds, UNFPA prepositions emergency supplies in high-risk coastal provinces
(mainly in Cabo Delgado, Nampula and Maputo), including SRH tents, Inter Agency Reproductive Health (IARH) kits, and
dignity kits, to maintain continuity of services. UNFPA’s strong operational presence in the aforementioned provinces is
supported by fully equipped teams ready for immediate deployment and to support the humanitarian response in the
districts.

UNHCR

UNICEF

For evacuation, WFP has supported INGD to map potential temporary accommodation centers, safe evacuation routes
and do community-level evacuation simulations ahead of the cyclone season.
For anticipatory food assistance, WFP has worked with INGD to map the capacity and prioritization of temporary
accommodation centers in relevant locations. Furthermore, WFP has mapped and identified retailers that could provide
commodity vouchers for early response phase..

important given the different potential scenarios:

If the action trigger is activated, agencies are authorized to use all the allocated funding.

Considerations
Both the geographical scope and the impact of cyclones are inherently difficult to predict despite the use of the best available
scientific data. As a result, it is possible that an event causes or exacerbates existing humanitarian needs despite not
formally reaching the thresholds required to utilize the pre-arranged funding. In this case, the RC/HC may request the ERC
to make an allocation through CERF’s Rapid Response window and use the projects already developed for the present
framework, thus reducing the administrative burden for all partners and expediting the release of life-saving funds. This
would not affect the amount of funding pre-arranged for this framework.
In general, having an anticipatory action framework supported by CERF neither precludes (nor guarantees) additional CERF
Rapid Response funding to a country for the same or other shocks.

9. Annex
Core principles
Each anticipatory action framework is tailored to the local context. OCHA has identified nine core principles that should
apply to the framework in Mozambique:
•

Prioritizing a multi-sectoral approach: Partners should prioritize multi-sectoral, co-targeted interventions, when
possible, to maximize synergies and to achieve great impact.

•

Ensuring the Centrality of Protection: In line with the IASC Centrality of Protection policy, and the principle of
“leaving no one behind” (2030 Agenda), all partners implementing anticipatory interventions have the responsibility
to ensure that their response will not aggravate the exposure of communities to risks of violence, insecurity,
extortion and exploitation. Anticipatory actions must also ensure that they benefit all communities (local
communities, displaced communities, returnees, and refugees) based on their needs, with due attention given to
obstacles linked to gender, age, disability or social affiliation.

•

Ensuring gender equality is central to inclusive and accountable anticipatory action: In line with IASC Policy
on Gender Equality and the Empowerment of Women and Girls in Humanitarian Action, all partners are responsible
and accountable to ensure humanitarian action, including anticipatory action is: rights-based; recognizes genderbased and intersectional needs, as well as potential gender-based discrimination in access to resources and
services; facilitate leadership and contribution of crisis-affected women and girls in the planning, implementation,
monitoring and evaluation of anticipatory actions and early response, that directly impact their lives and those of
their dependents and wider communities.

•

Promoting accountability to affected populations and the Protection from Sexual Exploitation and Abuse
(PSEA): Partners engaged in the implementation of the anticipatory action framework are responsible for the
integration of accountability to affected populations approaches in their activities. This includes making all efforts to
solicit, hear, and act upon the voices and priorities of affected people (including the most marginalized and at-risk
women, men, girls and boys) in a coordinated manner, before, during, and after anticipatory action. It also means
ensuring that community feedback leads to corrective action in future anticipatory action.

•

Supporting localization: All partners involved in the implementation of this framework are expected to honor the
HCT’s commitment towards localization. Equitable partnerships with local actors building upon their long-term
relationships and trust with communities during anticipatory action are crucial.

•

Engaging in partnerships: Collaboration across humanitarian actors is key to ensuring that all sectors are
engaged, and that assistance is mobilized according to the AA framework.

•

Generating development and climate adaptation co-benefits: Pilot participants are encouraged to consider how
financing for anticipatory action can complement financing for development by reducing suffering and addressing
the root causes of problems while supporting adaptation to climate change.

•

Capturing learning: Monitoring, evaluation and learning activities should be embedded at every phase of the
development and implementation of the AA framework.

•

Striving for integration: The framework seeks to integrate the anticipatory action activities into the existing
humanitarian architecture and to foster linkages with long term programming, including resilience and development
initiatives.

Trigger Monitoring Methodology
The exposed area is determined based on the forecast using the cone of uncertainty, accounting for the uncertainty in
location, and the wind contours.
•

Using the uncertainty cone and the forecasted center of the storm, the exposed area is calculated based on the
wind speed at each forecast point and extends inland only as far as the storm’s intensity meets the 119km/h
threshold. The uncertainty cone is truncated at points where the storm intensity drops below the threshold, with
interpolation between forecast points ensuring a smooth representation of wind speeds. Additional buffers are
applied around the truncated uncertainty cone based on the maximum wind contour for the threshold at each point.
• For both the action and observational wind speed trigger, one threshold is applied, resulting in one exposed areafor
tropical cyclones.
