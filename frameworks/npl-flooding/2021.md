# Nepal Flood — 2021 (pilot)

> No DS code repo exists for this pilot; the trigger is taken from the public framework PDF (Anticipation Hub / ReliefWeb), whose full text was fetched and verified against this page during QA, plus corroborating OCHA/CERF/Centre for Humanitarian Data material. This page is a sourced institutional summary, not a derivation from code — see `discrepancies` for what remains outstanding (chiefly the per-basin funding split and the not-yet-saved raw/ extract).

## Summary

The Nepal Anticipatory Action Framework Pilot was OCHA's first anticipatory action framework for floods in Nepal, developed by the Centre for Humanitarian Data with the Resident Coordinator's Office (RCO) Nepal and endorsed by the Emergency Relief Coordinator in September 2021 (framework document published 9 August 2021). It ran independent two-stage (readiness/action) triggers for two river systems — the **Koshi Basin** (monitored at Chatara station, eastern Nepal) and the **Karnali, Babai and West Rapti Basins** (monitored at Chisapani station, western Nepal) — using GloFAS forecasts and Nepal's Department of Hydrology and Meteorology (DHM) flood-warning system. On trigger, CERF pre-arranged up to **$6.7 million**, released automatically per basin, for five UN agencies (UNFPA, UNICEF, UN Women, WFP, WHO) working with the Nepal Red Cross Society and national NGOs to deliver cash and relief assistance to over **80,000 people** across 23 flood-prone municipalities ("palikas") in five districts. The pilot was designed to run for two years from endorsement, with one severe-flooding event pre-arranged per basin. It did not activate during the 2021 monsoon (a readiness trigger was reached at Chatara in August 2021 but did not escalate to action), then recorded its first real activation in the Karnali basin in October 2022. It was subsequently extended for the 2023 and 2024 monsoon seasons before being substantially revised in later framework versions.

## Method

Two parallel, basin-specific pipelines feed the same two-stage trigger design:

1. **GloFAS (readiness, both basins):** The ECMWF/Copernicus Global Flood Awareness System provides ensemble river-discharge forecasts. RCO Nepal, supported by OCHA's Centre for Humanitarian Data, monitors GloFAS daily during the monsoon. The readiness trigger (Stage I) fires when the GloFAS forecast predicts a **70% likelihood of discharge exceeding the 1-in-2-year return period (RP2)** at the relevant station, with up to 7 days' lead time.
2. **DHM flood-warning system (action, both basins):** The action trigger (Stage II) requires a **DHM flood-warning bulletin/special alert** for the basin, AND EITHER the GloFAS 3-day forecast repeating the 70%-at-RP2 condition, OR the observed river level crossing the government-defined "Danger Level" at the station. Lead time ranges from 3 hours to 3 days.

RCO/CHD notify the UN agencies and CERF by email as each trigger is reached. Per the framework's activation protocol, the **readiness trigger precipitates the disbursement of CERF funds** (and launches preparatory activities), while the **action trigger precipitates the commencement of the anticipatory response delivery** to affected people. Because GloFAS can reach the action threshold without a readiness trigger firing first, the doc provides that CERF instead disburses as soon as the action trigger is reached in that case. The Koshi and Karnali systems operate and fund independently of one another.

## Trigger logic

- **Keys off:** GloFAS ensemble discharge forecast (readiness, both basins); DHM flood-warning bulletin/special alert plus GloFAS 3-day forecast or observed danger-level crossing (action, both basins).
- **Decision rule (plain language):** For each basin independently: if GloFAS's 7-day forecast gives ≥70% probability of discharge exceeding the basin's 1-in-2-year level, the readiness trigger fires — CERF disburses the pre-arranged funds and agencies launch preparatory activities. If DHM subsequently issues a flood-warning bulletin for that basin AND either GloFAS's 3-day forecast repeats the 70%-at-RP2 condition or the river is observed crossing the government danger level, the action trigger fires and agencies commence delivery of assistance to affected people. (If the action threshold is reached without a readiness trigger first — a real possibility given GloFAS's limited lead-time skill — CERF disburses at the action trigger instead.)
- **Activation structure:** Two independent basin-level systems (Koshi/Chatara and Karnali/Babai/West Rapti/Chisapani), each a two-stage readiness→action design. No cross-basin dependency — either basin can trigger on its own.
- **Calibration:** Nominal threshold is the 1-in-2-year (RP2) GloFAS discharge at 70% forecast probability — in absolute terms **6,350 m³/s at Chatara** and **6,300 m³/s at Chisapani**, with DHM government danger levels of **7.0 m at Chatara** and **10.8 m at Chisapani**. Because GloFAS underestimates observed discharge, the pilot analysis found the EFFECTIVE return period is higher (lower-frequency, higher-impact) than nominal: the PDF states ~1-in-2.5-year for the Chatara **action** trigger and ~1-in-6-year for the Chisapani **action** trigger, adding that both Chisapani triggers correspond to higher-impact events (without pinning a separate figure to the Chisapani readiness). Historically the Chisapani forecast had never reached the RP2 threshold at 4–7-day lead times.
- **Authoritative source:** the 2021 pilot framework PDF (`framework_doc`), published on ReliefWeb 9 August 2021; endorsed by the ERC in September 2021. No repo implements this trigger.
- **Operated by:** RCO Nepal, supported by OCHA's Centre for Humanitarian Data. No automated monitoring pipeline; manual daily monitoring of GloFAS and DHM outputs.

## Monitoring

Monitored manually by RCO Nepal with support from OCHA's Centre for Humanitarian Data, daily during the monsoon season, watching GloFAS forecasts for both Chatara and Chisapani stations and DHM's flood-warning bulletins. On trigger, RCO/CHD notify the UN implementing agencies and CERF by email. No deployed app or automated monitoring pipeline is associated with this version; cross-reference `infrastructure/deployments.md` (no Azure/GH Pages entry for this framework).

## Historical activations

**One real (funded) activation on record under this version's design; one near-trigger that did not escalate:**

1. **October 2022 — Karnali Basin (first-ever activation):** Warnings of heavy flooding in Kailali, Bardiya and Banke districts triggered the readiness stage on 2 October 2022; the action stage triggered 7 October 2022; the river crossed the danger level at Chisapani on 9 October 2022. CERF authorized **$3.2 million** for UNFPA, UN Women and WFP to deliver multi-purpose cash, relief kits and reproductive-health/GBV services ahead of peak flooding. This was the first-ever activation of the Nepal AA pilot, and only spent the Karnali basin's envelope (the Koshi/Chatara system remained available). ([Centre for Humanitarian Data](https://centre.humdata.org/triggering-anticipatory-action-for-floods-in-nepal/))
2. **August 2021 — Koshi Basin (readiness trigger reached, no activation):** On 13 August 2021, GloFAS forecast a 73% chance of the Chatara station exceeding its 1-in-2-year discharge level on 18 August, meeting the readiness-trigger threshold. Per the Centre for Humanitarian Data's own account, the action trigger was not subsequently reached and the pilot was **not activated** during the 2021 monsoon — no CERF funds were released. Recorded here as a near-trigger for context, not as a real activation.

The pilot was later extended for the 2023 and 2024 monsoon seasons (per the successor 2025-08-25 page's frontmatter) before being substantially revised — narrowing scope, raising thresholds, and eventually dropping GloFAS from the action trigger entirely by 2025.

## Key decisions & rationale

**Why two independent basin systems instead of one national trigger?** Koshi (east) and Karnali/Babai/West Rapti (west) are hydrologically and administratively distinct systems monitored at different DHM stations (Chatara, Chisapani); designing independent readiness/action triggers per basin let each fire (and fund) on its own without one basin's flood risk being diluted by the other's forecast.

**Why a nominal RP2 threshold at 70% probability despite the bias correction showing much higher effective return periods at Chisapani?** The pilot analysis found GloFAS systematically underestimates observed discharge, especially at Chisapani, but used the same nominal RP2/70% design across both basins for simplicity and government/DHM alignment, accepting that the Chisapani trigger would in practice require a substantially larger event (effective ~1-in-6-year) than the Chatara trigger (effective ~1-in-2.5-year) to fire.

**Why compound the action trigger with DHM bulletin AND (GloFAS OR danger level)?** This let the trigger draw on both a forecast signal (GloFAS 3-day) and a real-time observational signal (government danger-level gauge reading) as alternate paths to action, while requiring the government's own DHM bulletin as a floor condition for government ownership and alignment — a design the later 2025 revision moved away from (dropping GloFAS from the action trigger entirely; see `2025-08-25.md`).
