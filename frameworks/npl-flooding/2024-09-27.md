# Nepal Flood — 2024-09-27

> No persisted full-text extraction of this version's PDF exists yet (`raw_extract: []`), but in QA review the primary framework PDF (anticipation-hub copy) was fetched and its trigger design, funding, scope, and validity were confirmed against it (see Open questions for the residual items and Sources for what was checked).

## Summary

The Nepal Anticipatory Action Framework for Floods (2024 revision) coordinated humanitarian pre-action for communities at risk of severe monsoon flooding in **both of Nepal's major flood-prone river basins**: the Koshi (eastern Nepal) and the Karnali (western Nepal). Each basin had its own two-stage trigger — a GloFAS-based readiness trigger (up to 7-day lead) followed by a compound DHM-danger-level/GloFAS action trigger (3 hours to 3 days' lead) — and its own independently-triggerable share of a pre-arranged CERF envelope of up to $6.5 million. On activation, FAO, UNFPA, UNICEF and WFP, working with the Nepal Red Cross Society and local partners, delivered multi-purpose cash and other assistance to up to 238,831 people across 15 municipalities in four districts (Sunsari, Saptari in the east; Bardiya, Kailali in the west). This version extended a framework first piloted in 2021 (and revised in 2022/2023) through the 2024 monsoon season; it fired once, in the Koshi basin, in autumn 2024, and was itself superseded by a narrower, Koshi-only 2025 revision.

## Method

Two independent basin-level pipelines fed the same two-stage decision structure:

1. **GloFAS (readiness):** The ECMWF/Copernicus Global Flood Awareness System 7-day ensemble discharge forecast was monitored for Chatara station (Koshi) and Chisapani station (Karnali). The readiness trigger fired per basin when the 7-day forecast predicted ≥70% probability of reaching the 1-in-2-year return period (RP2). The 2024 PDF expresses the threshold as a *return period*, not an absolute discharge; the RP2 discharge equivalents per the Copernicus dashboard (stored in the repo's `src/constants.py`) are **8,113 m³/s at Chatara** and **5,664 m³/s at Chisapani** (loosely, ~8,100 / ~5,700 m³/s).
2. **DHM + GloFAS (action):** The action trigger was a compound, per-basin criterion: it fired if EITHER the government Department of Hydrology and Meteorology's real-time gauge reached the government-defined danger level (a real-time observational signal, giving as little as a 3-hour warning) OR the GloFAS 3-day forecast reached ≥70% probability of the RP2 threshold (a forecast signal, giving up to 3 days' lead).

CERF funds were pre-arranged for **one severe flooding event per river basin** — i.e. Koshi and Karnali each had their own budget line, so one basin firing did not exhaust the other's capacity.

## Trigger logic

- **Keys off:** GloFAS 7-day ensemble discharge forecast (readiness, both basins); DHM real-time danger-level gauge reading OR GloFAS 3-day forecast (action, both basins).
- **Decision rule (plain language):** In each basin, when GloFAS shows ≥70% of ensemble members crossing the 1-in-2-year discharge within 7 days, the readiness trigger fires and agencies begin preparatory activities for that basin. The action trigger then fires — separately per basin — the moment either the DHM gauge crosses the government danger level in real time, or a subsequent GloFAS 3-day forecast still shows ≥70% probability of RP2; agencies then deliver assistance in that basin.
- **Activation structure:** Two basins (Koshi, Karnali) × two stages (readiness, action) = four windows, each independently triggerable; a trigger in one basin did not consume the other basin's pre-arranged share.
- **Calibration:** GloFAS thresholds set at the 1-in-2-year return period (RP2) at each station, at 70% ensemble-probability. The PDF notes that, owing to GloFAS forecast bias, the nominal RP2 design actually corresponds to lower-frequency/higher-impact events — an effective ~1-in-2.5-year event for the Chatara action trigger and ~1-in-6-year for the Chisapani action trigger. The DHM danger-level component is an absolute, government-defined river-stage threshold (7.0 m at Chatara, 10.5 m at Chisapani), not return-period calibrated — the exact hydrological equivalence between "danger level" and a return period is not stated.
- **Authoritative source:** 2024 framework PDF (`framework_doc`), endorsed/dated 2024-09-27 per the successor page's `supersedes` field; listed on ReliefWeb as published 2024-10-01.
- **Operated by:** CHD and RCO Nepal (jointly), per the pattern documented for the 2025 successor; no automated monitoring pipeline — monitoring was manual.

## Historical activations

**One confirmed real activation under this version:**

- **September–October 2024 — Koshi Basin (action trigger):** Following the action trigger firing on heavy monsoon flooding along the Koshi river, CERF released **$3.4 million** — reportedly within 6 minutes of activation. FAO, UNFPA, UNICEF and WFP delivered digital cash assistance, WASH supplies, food, and gender-based-violence/SRH services to communities in Sunsari and Saptari districts. This was a **partial (Koshi-only) activation** — the Karnali basin's share of the envelope was not triggered under this version.

A separate Karnali (Chisapani) activation in **October 2022** ($3.2M CERF) is documented elsewhere in this KB ([2025 sibling page](2025-08-25.md)) but occurred under the **prior** (2022/addendum) version of this framework, before this 2024-09-27 version was endorsed — it is not recorded here (see `extra.prior_karnali_activation`).

## Key decisions & rationale

**Why cover two basins with independent budgets?** Nepal's two major flood-prone basins (Koshi in the east, Karnali in the west) are hydrologically unconnected, so the framework treated them as separate risk pools, each with its own readiness/action trigger pair and its own share of the pre-arranged CERF envelope — allowing one basin to activate without exhausting the other's capacity (`all_in: false`).

**Why a compound (DHM-or-GloFAS) action trigger?** Combining a real-time government gauge reading with a short-range GloFAS forecast gave the action stage two independent paths to fire — a fast-but-late observational signal (as little as 3 hours' warning) and a slower-but-earlier forecast signal (up to 3 days) — maximizing the chance of catching a fast-onset flood event while still preferring the earlier forecast lead time when available. The 2025 revision later dropped the GloFAS leg entirely on DHM's advice that GloFAS did not reliably reflect flood severity (see [2025 page](2025-08-25.md)).

**Why flat 15,000 NPR cash transfers?** WFP and UNICEF used a single flat per-household rate rather than a needs-adjusted scale; the 2025 revision replaced this with a household-size-based rate aligned to Nepal's Shock-Responsive Social Protection standard (adopted after this version was endorsed).
