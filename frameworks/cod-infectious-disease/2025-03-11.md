# DRC Cholera — 2025-03-11

## Summary

The DRC cholera anticipatory action framework triggers on observational epidemiological data — specifically, an anomalous number of suspected cholera cases, deaths, or case-incidence in a single health zone for three consecutive weeks, with laboratory confirmation — to pre-position CERF funding (up to $750,000 per activation, max $3M total over 2 years) for UNICEF and WHO rapid-response activities. The framework has two scenarios differentiated by geography: Scenario 1 targets the five endemic provinces (Nord-Kivu, Sud-Kivu, Tanganyika, Haut-Lomami, Haut-Katanga); Scenario 2 targets all non-endemic provinces. Each scenario can trigger independently. The framework was first endorsed in December 2022, operated as a pilot through two activations in 2023, and was revised and re-endorsed on 11 March 2025 with lessons incorporated.

## Method

Weekly epidemiological data are sourced from PNECHOL-MD (Programme National pour l'Élimination du Choléra et le Contrôle des Autres Maladies Diarrhéiques), the national cholera programme. Two streams are used: a national aggregate database (health zone + epidemiological week, updated weekly, with a 2-3 week lag) and a real-time provincial database for the five endemic provinces that reduces the data lag.

For each health zone and each of three indicators (suspected cases, deaths, cases per population), two conditions are assessed each week:

1. **Percentile condition**: where does the current weekly value sit in the historical distribution for that type of province? Thresholds define four alert levels (0–3) based on percentile bands (0–50 / 50–95 / 95–99 / >99).
2. **Rate-of-increase condition**: how does the current week compare to the previous week? Four levels (0–3) based on multipliers (<2x / 2–3x / 3–4x / >4x).

This yields six criteria per health zone per week (3 indicators × 2 conditions). The maximum alert level across all six criteria is taken. If that maximum is Level 3 for three consecutive weeks, and at least one suspected case in that health zone was laboratory-confirmed in the prior month, the trigger is reached.

The percentile baseline is calculated separately for endemic and non-endemic provinces and is updated quarterly. The Centre for Humanitarian Data (OCHA CHD) runs the monitoring and sends an alert notification when the threshold is reached; WHO, UNICEF, or the HC can waive activation within 24 hours; otherwise CERF automatically disburses funds.

## Trigger logic

- **Keys off:** Weekly PNECHOL-MD suspected cholera cases, deaths, and case-incidence (cases/population) at health-zone level.
- **Decision rule (plain language):** Any health zone reaches Alert Level 3 (value above the 99th historical percentile for its province type, or more than 4x the previous week's value) on at least one of the six indicator-condition combinations, for three consecutive weeks, with at least one lab-confirmed case in the preceding month, and no activation in the same or a neighboring province within the past six months.
- **Activation structure:** Two independent scenarios (endemic vs. non-endemic provinces). Each can trigger separately. A 6-month spatiotemporal cooldown prevents re-activation in the same province cluster after any activation.
- **Calibration:** Alert Level 3 = above the 99th percentile or >4x week-over-week increase. Percentile computed over the historical distribution of non-zero weekly values, separately for endemic and non-endemic health zones, updated quarterly. The 2022 doc specifies an EXPANDING baseline (all data since January 2020, re-fitted every quarter); the 2025 doc keeps the quarterly-update methodology. NOTE the live monitor does not actually re-fit quarterly — it uses a frozen 2022-09-30 thresholds file plus hardcoded absolute thresholds (see Discrepancies). The IDSR branch explores a longer 2006-present baseline (with quarterly updates per its own recommendation; the rolling-5yr-excl-2020-21 / p98.8 figures are validation-grid sweeps, not the recommended operational config).
- **Authoritative source:** The 2025 revised framework PDF (`framework_doc`). The repo (`code_ref`) implements and extends this for analysis; the IDSR branch is exploring a data-source migration not yet endorsed.
- **Operated by:** OCHA CHD runs the monitoring pipeline and trigger alert. No external party operates the live trigger.

## Monitoring

OCHA CHD monitors the trigger weekly. The monitoring script (`src/monitor_dse.R`) runs against PNECHOL-MD data from the DSE (Disease Surveillance E-platform) database. On trigger, an email alert is sent to UNICEF, WHO, OCHA DRC, OCHA HQ (CERF, HFSA, OAD), OCHA regional office (ROWCA), PNECHOL-MD, and the HC/RC. Partners have 24 hours to raise objections; otherwise CERF disburses automatically. A GitHub Pages-hosted Quarto evaluation book for the IDSR data evaluation is published at the URL in `apps`.

## Key decisions & rationale

- **Why observational rather than forecast-based:** Epidemiological forecasting models for cholera were evaluated and found insufficiently reliable as triggers. The design deliberately uses near-real-time surveillance data to catch early outbreak signals, not a prospective forecast. The "anticipatory" character comes from acting before a small cluster becomes an uncontrolled epidemic, not from acting before any cases appear.
- **Why the percentile + rate-of-increase composite:** Sorbonne University (a scientific partner) proposed this dual-condition system to handle two different epidemiological situations: a spike in a zone that normally has zero cases (rate-of-increase criterion catches this even if the absolute count is low), and a high-case zone showing unusual acceleration (percentile criterion catches this). The combination ensures the trigger is neither too sensitive to baseline-endemic zones nor too blind to sudden epidemic provinces.
- **Why 3 consecutive weeks:** Requiring 3 weeks at Level 3 reduces false positives from single-week noise while remaining responsive enough to anticipate large outbreaks. Lab confirmation (at least one confirmed case in the prior month) adds a quality gate on the data signal.
- **Why endemic/non-endemic split:** The endemic provinces (eastern DRC, conflict-affected) have persistent cholera transmission at baseline; percentiles must be computed separately so that a "high" value in Nord-Kivu is not swamped by its normal endemic burden, and vice versa for epidemic provinces where any cluster is anomalous.
- **Why 6-month spatiotemporal cooldown:** Budget constraint (max 4 activations over 2 years) and operational capacity — simultaneous activations in neighboring provinces would overwhelm implementing partners. The cooldown prevents a single epidemic event from generating multiple sequential activations.
- **PNECHOL vs IDSR:** PNECHOL-MD is authoritative, validated, and the source of record; IDSR provides national data since 2006 (vs 3 years for PNECHOL) and is publicly accessible (PNECHOL is confidential). The IDSR branch is investigating whether 20 years of baseline provides more stable percentile thresholds. High agreement (r=0.987-0.996 nationally, 0 false IDSR triggers) supports eventual replacement, conditional on resolving 35 unmatched health zones and the 2021 underreporting gap in Sud-Kivu.

## Changes from previous version

The 2025 revision (superseding the December 2022 pilot) made the following changes:

1. **Dropped Scenario 2 (external shock trigger):** The original 2022 design included a third scenario triggered by a CERF rapid-response disbursement for an external shock (flood, volcanic eruption, displacement) in an endemic province. This was removed in 2025. The revised framework has only two scenarios, both epidemiological, split by endemic/non-endemic geography.
2. **Incorporated lessons from 2023 activations:** The two 2023 activations provided operational evidence that informed the revision, though the specific technical changes to the trigger logic are not elaborated in the 2025 PDF. The alert-level table and 3-week consecutive threshold appear unchanged.
3. **Updated implementing partner details:** The 2025 framework adds VIPATU (UNICEF partner), ADRA, MDA (Médecins d'Afrique), and Alima as named NGO partners for UNICEF and WHO activities.
4. **Strengthened protection language:** A new principle on "centralité de la protection" was added, addressing gender, age, disability, and community-type equity in the response.
5. **Budget structure maintained:** $3M CERF over 2 years, $750K per activation, max 4 activations — same as the original.
