# LAC Dry Corridor Drought — 2026-03-13

> The canonical trigger is the code at `code_ref`; this page explains it, it does not redefine it.
> **Note:** Three separate country PDFs share a common design. The Guatemala framework doc is listed as `framework_doc` (primary); El Salvador and Honduras are in `framework_doc_annexes`. All three were endorsed by the ERC on 2026-03-13.

## Summary

The Central American Dry Corridor (CADC) anticipatory action framework covers drought in three countries — Guatemala, Honduras, and El Salvador — using a shared trigger design calibrated independently per country. ECMWF SEAS5 seasonal precipitation forecasts are aggregated over prioritized sub-national areas and compared against empirically-derived return-period thresholds. Two monitoring windows track the Primera (MJJA, May–August) and Postrera (SON, September–November) rainy seasons. When any leadtime threshold is breached in either window, CERF pre-arranged funding is released immediately to FAO, PAHO, UNICEF, and WFP. The 2026 version is the second generation of the framework, directly incorporating lessons from the July 2025 Postrera activation in Guatemala — the first collective, joint, multisectoral AA activation in the Dry Corridor. This version has already triggered for Guatemala, El Salvador, and Honduras in March 2026 (a three-country simultaneous activation) — see Historical activations.

## Method

1. **Data source:** ECMWF SEAS5 seasonal precipitation hindcasts and forecasts (1991–2024 baseline). For Guatemala, INSIVUMEH national forecasts were used in the 2024–2025 pilot but the 2026 framework explicitly moves to ECMWF SEAS5 as the primary monitoring source; INSIVUMEH remains involved for consultation but is no longer the operational trigger data source.
2. **Spatial aggregation:** Forecasts are extracted over prioritized admin-1 departments (GTM: Chiquimula, Zacapa, Jalapa, El Progreso; HND: El Paraíso, Francisco Morazán; SLV: Ahuachapán, Santa Ana — national average for SLV). Aggregation uses area-weighted zonal means.
3. **Threshold calibration:** A grid search over all possible per-leadtime return-period combinations (RP 4–20 at each leadtime) is applied to the 1991–2024 hindcast record. Candidate configurations are restricted to those where longer leadtimes use equal or stricter thresholds, filtered to those with combined annual RP in the 3–5 year range, and then further filtered to ensure the mechanism would have activated in "Priority 1" years (CERF drought allocations in 2009, 2014, 2015, 2018). Final selection uses Weibull plotting positions for return periods and maximizes a tolerated ERA5-based F1 score (forecast vs. observed seasonal anomaly) while penalizing high inter-leadtime threshold variance.
4. **Decision rule:** Trigger fires if the aggregated seasonal precipitation forecast falls at or below the pre-set return-period threshold at any monitored leadtime in either season. Any window activation releases the full CERF envelope for that country.
5. **Monitoring cadence:** ECMWF SEAS5 is published on the 5th of each month; the CHD automated system issues an email update to country stakeholders the same day.

## Trigger logic

- **Keys off:** ECMWF SEAS5 seasonal precipitation forecast aggregated over priority departments/zones, expressed in mm per season.
- **Decision rule (plain language):** On the 5th of each month during the monitoring window (March–May for Primera; July–September for HND/GTM, August–September for SLV Postrera), the CHD system computes the aggregated seasonal forecast and compares it against the pre-set threshold for that leadtime and country. If the forecast is at or below the threshold (i.e., indicates drought at the specified return period), the trigger fires, the RC is notified, and the CERF secretariat immediately releases pre-arranged funds to the four implementing agencies.
- **Activation structure:** Two windows; any single leadtime breach within a window activates the full envelope. Both windows use the same CERF budget — if Window A fires, all funds are released; they remain reserved for Window B if Window A does not fire.
- **Calibration:** Empirical return periods using Weibull plotting positions on the 1991–2024 SEAS5 hindcast. The target combined annual RP is approximately 1 in 3 years (1 in 2.92). Individual per-leadtime and per-season return periods vary; see the reconciliation note in Discrepancies below regarding inconsistency in the GTM PDF.
- **Authoritative source:** The latest framework PDFs (framework_doc + annexes). The repo (code_ref) implements and derives the thresholds; actual numeric thresholds are stored in a blob parquet file.
- **Operated by:** OCHA Centre for Humanitarian Data (CHD) operates the automated monthly monitoring system. The RC retains discretion to suspend an activation in exceptional circumstances after CHD notification.

## Monitoring

- **System:** OCHA CHD automated monitoring. ECMWF SEAS5 is published on the 5th of each month; CHD issues an email update the same day during the monitoring windows (March–September).
- **For Guatemala:** The 2024–2025 system required a manual two-step process because INSIVUMEH forecast data was shared informally by email (not via automated pipeline) and had to be manually uploaded to blob storage before a final status email could be sent. The 2026 framework removes this dependency — ECMWF SEAS5 is the sole trigger source, making full automation possible.
- **Code:** `src/monitoring_2025/update_activation_status.R` is the current operational script; it loads the threshold parquet from blob, fetches SEAS5 zonal stats from the PostgreSQL DB, and sends an email with trigger status and a map.
- **Cadence:** Monthly, on the 5th, during the March–September monitoring period.

## Historical activations

**2025 (first activation — GTM Postrera):** In July 2025, INSIVUMEH's Postrera seasonal forecast (June 2025 publication) fell below the RP-4 threshold for Guatemala's Chiquimula Postrera window. CERF released USD 4 million in under two hours. FAO, PAHO, UNICEF, and WFP reached approximately 51,500 people in Jocotán and Camotán (Chiquimula department) before the peak drought impact. This was the Dry Corridor's first collective, joint, multisectoral anticipatory action activation.

**2026 (second activation — GTM + SLV + HND Primera, three countries):** On 5 March 2026, ECMWF SEAS5 forecasts for MJJA (Window A) reached the pre-agreed thresholds in Guatemala, El Salvador **and Honduras** simultaneously. CERF released USD 4 million (GTM, 60,000 people, four departments), USD 2.5 million (SLV, first-ever AA activation for El Salvador, 20,000 people in Ahuachapán and Santa Ana) and USD 4 million (HND, first-ever AA activation for Honduras, up to 65,000 people in El Paraíso and Francisco Morazán) — USD 10.5 million total for up to 145,000 people across the three countries ([OCHA / ReliefWeb](https://reliefweb.int/report/honduras/anticipatory-action-honduras-2026-activation)).

No activation occurred in Guatemala or any other country during the 2024 Primera or Postrera monitoring windows.

## Key decisions & rationale

- **Why move from INSIVUMEH to ECMWF for Guatemala:** The 2025 activation was delayed because INSIVUMEH's institutional changes and model updates created gaps in the data pipeline, reducing the implementation window. ECMWF SEAS5, being available automatically on the 5th of each month, eliminates this dependency. INSIVUMEH remains a consultation partner.
- **Why RP 1 in 3 years (combined annual):** The grid-search calibration was constrained to designs that activate in all Priority 1 years (CERF drought allocations in 2009, 2014, 2015, 2018). The 1-in-3 annual target reflects the balance between sensitivity (catching severe droughts) and avoiding overly frequent activations that would exhaust CERF funds and erode partner capacity.
- **Why per-leadtime thresholds tighten at longer lead times:** Longer forecasts have lower skill; requiring a higher RP at longer lead time means the mechanism is less likely to fire on a false alarm with a 3-month-ahead forecast than with a 1-month-ahead forecast.
- **Why El Salvador scope shifted from San Vicente to Ahuachapán/Santa Ana:** Climate analysis 2021–2025 identified western El Salvador (Ahuachapán, Santa Ana) as having higher drought risk and stronger ECMWF forecast skill for the framework's target windows. San Vicente had been included in the 2024–2025 pilot for capacity reasons.
- **Why Guatemala expanded to four departments:** Lessons from the 2025 activation showed that Chiquimula-only coverage missed drought-affected populations in adjacent Zacapa, Jalapa, and El Progreso. The expanded AOI improves humanitarian coverage while staying within operational feasibility.
- **Funds per window vs. per season:** The CERF envelope is identical for both windows. If Window A fires, all funds are released regardless of whether Window B would also have fired. This was a deliberate simplicity choice to reduce administrative burden; the downside is that a Primera activation consumes the budget before a potentially worse Postrera drought.

## Changes from previous version

The 2024–2025 pilot framework (version superseded) included:
- **Guatemala only (Chiquimula):** One department vs. four in 2026.
- **El Salvador San Vicente vs. Ahuachapán/Santa Ana:** AOI shifted west based on climate analysis update.
- **INSIVUMEH as co-trigger for Guatemala:** Replaced by ECMWF SEAS5-only in 2026 to remove the manual data ingestion bottleneck.
- **Honduras Postrera monitoring window:** Changed from June–August to July–September (LT2/LT1/LT0) after reviewing forecast skill.
- **El Salvador Postrera monitoring window:** Changed from June–August to August–September (two months instead of three).
- **Calibration methodology:** The 2026 version uses a more rigorous grid-search approach with dual F1 scoring (ERA5-based and EM-DAT-based) plus Priority 1/2 year filtering. The 2024–2025 pilot used a simpler RP-4 quantile approach on the raw INSIVUMEH/ECMWF historical record.
- **Geographic scope validation:** Three additional departments added to GTM based on activation learnings.
- **Joint targeting and AAP mechanisms:** Formalized following the 2025 experience; WFP designated lead for targeting, FAO designated lead for evaluation, UNICEF designated lead for AAP.

## Open questions / known issues

Nicaragua had no confirmed standalone endorsement or CERF pre-arrangement of its own, so it is not tracked as a separate framework page.
- **Numeric thresholds:** Table 3 in all three PDFs (actual mm thresholds by leadtime and country) is embedded as an image and not extractable via pdftotext. These should be retrieved from the blob parquet `df_thresholds_seas5_insivumeh_adm1_refined_insiv_update_202506.parquet` for future reference.
- **2026 calibration code:** The Weibull/ROC-AUC/F1 grid-search code described in Annex 2 of the 2026 PDFs does not appear on the active branch. It may exist on a separate branch or was run externally at CHD.
- **INSIVUMEH 2026 role:** The framework states INSIVUMEH "remains actively involved" for consultation. The exact protocol for when INSIVUMEH information would override or supplement the ECMWF trigger is not specified.
