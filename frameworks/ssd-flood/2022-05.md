# South Sudan Flood — 2022-05

> **Historical anticipatory pilot, NOT a formal/endorsed AA framework.** OCHA's own documentation states the 2022 action was an anticipatory CERF allocation taken *without* a formalised framework or pre-agreed quantitative trigger. This page records that pilot from public OCHA/CERF sources; the repo-based exploratory work toward a possible *future* trigger lives at [`analysis/ssd-flooding.md`](../../analysis/ssd-flooding.md). See `extra.classification_note`.

## Summary
In May 2022 CERF released US$15 million anticipatorily — months earlier than the 2020 and 2021 flood responses — ahead of the projected 2022 rainy-season floods in South Sudan, complemented by US$4 million from the South Sudan Humanitarian Fund (SSHF). The funds concentrated on the Bentiu IDP camp and surrounding areas in Unity State, where more than 320,000 people faced a high risk of flooding. Crucially, this was **not** a formal anticipatory action framework: OCHA explored available forecasts in March 2022 and concluded they were not reliable enough to build a formal trigger, so it piloted a risk-analysis-based early-action project instead. Partners constructed and reinforced over 55 km of dykes and invested early in WASH and cholera prevention, reaching 301,649 people and reportedly preventing the evacuation of around 100,000 people. The pilot was a one-off and was not repeated as a standing framework.

## Method
OCHA and the Centre for Humanitarian Data examined whether forecasts could support a formal flood AA framework for South Sudan in early 2022. The Sudd wetlands / White Nile flood system proved too hard to forecast reliably (complex multi-year water storage, sparse hydrological and weather observation, and access/conflict constraints), so no quantitative trigger could be calibrated. Rather than not act, OCHA led a pilot "early action" project with partners: a CERF anticipatory allocation was released on the basis of risk analysis and seasonal expectations ahead of the rains, funding physical flood-protection and pre-positioning works (dykes, latrines, water treatment, cholera vaccination) in the most exposed location (Bentiu, Unity State) before peak flooding arrived.

## Trigger logic
- **Keys off:** No formal trigger. The decision keyed off risk analysis (historical flood exposure in Unity State / Bentiu) and seasonal forecast expectations, plus expert judgement — not a calibrated indicator/threshold.
- **Decision rule (plain language):** There was no pre-agreed quantitative rule. OCHA judged, ahead of the 2022 rainy season, that flooding in the already-flooded Bentiu area was sufficiently likely and severe to justify releasing CERF funds early, and CERF approved an anticipatory allocation.
- **Activation structure:** Single anticipatory allocation; no staged readiness/action windows, no AND/OR logic.
- **Calibration:** None (bespoke). No return period or percentile threshold was set; OCHA explicitly found forecasts insufficient for formal calibration.
- **Authoritative source:** The OCHA / Centre for Humanitarian Data learning report *Innovating Anticipatory Action: Lessons from the 2022 South Sudan Floods* (`framework_doc`, 18 Jan 2024) and the CERF allocation summary (22-RR-SSD-52855). There is no framework PDF because no framework was produced.
- **Operated by:** OCHA / OCHA South Sudan / CHD (the pilot decision and CERF process). No live monitoring platform.

## Historical activations
**One anticipatory allocation (May 2022).** CERF released US$15M (plus US$4M SSHF) ahead of the 2022 rainy-season floods for Unity State / Bentiu, reaching 301,649 people across WASH, Nutrition, Agriculture, Camp Management, Shelter, Health and Protection; funds built/reinforced 55+ km of dykes and supported early cholera prevention. This is recorded as the pilot's activation, but it was **not** fired by a formal AA trigger.

For context (not activations of this pilot): the 2020 and 2021 South Sudan flood responses were post-event CERF rapid response, and a later US$10M CERF rapid response (Sep 2024) was likewise post-event — none anticipatory.

## Key decisions & rationale
- **Why no formal framework?** OCHA explored forecasts in March 2022 and judged them too unreliable for the Sudd/White Nile flood system to support a calibrated trigger (complex hydrology, multi-year standing water, sparse data, conflict/access). The deliberate lesson OCHA drew is that the absence of a formal framework should not stop humanitarians from acting ahead of a predictable shock — risk analysis plus expert judgement can substitute where forecasting cannot.
- **Why Bentiu / Unity State?** It was among the most flood-exposed locations, with a large IDP population already living amid standing water, so pre-emptive dyke and WASH works offered high marginal protection.
- **Why act early via CERF?** Releasing US$15M in May 2022 (vs. post-event in 2020/2021) allowed physical flood-protection works to be completed *before* peak flooding, reportedly averting ~100,000 evacuations and a public-health emergency.
