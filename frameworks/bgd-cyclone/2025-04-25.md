# Bangladesh Tropical Cyclone — 2025-04-25

## Summary

The Bangladesh cyclone anticipatory action framework, initiated in January 2023 and revised to its current form in April 2025, pre-arranges funding and activities for multi-sectoral humanitarian response ahead of severe cyclones making landfall on Bangladesh's Bay of Bengal coast. It activates when at least two of five forecast models agree that wind speeds will reach 118 km/h (Category I, ~1-in-4.75-year return period) in the six target coastal districts — Satkhira, Patuakhali, Bhola, Noakhali, Barguna, and Khulna — within a 3-to-5-day window (readiness) or up to 72-hour window (action). A third, observational trigger covers the scenario where a cyclone reaches threshold wind speed at landfall without having triggered the forecast-based stages; associated early-response activities can run for up to ~4 weeks after landfall. Total pre-arranged financing is USD 9,951,987 per the doc's own headline figure: a dedicated CERF envelope of USD 4 million plus USD 5,951,987 in co-funding (BDRCS, BRAC, STEP, UNFPA, UNICEF, Islamic Relief, Save the Children, Start Network, WFP) — though the co-funding line items in the same table actually sum to $6,015,987, a $64,000 gap against the printed $5,951,987 co-funding subtotal that the document does not explain (see discrepancies). The four CERF-funded UN implementing agencies are FAO, UNFPA, UNICEF, and WFP. The framework has not been activated for a real cyclone event as of ingestion date.

## Method

During cyclone seasons (April–June and October–November), a three-person monitoring team tracks five forecast systems — BMD, IMD, ECMWF, GFS, and JTWC — twice daily. When a cyclone threat develops, the team applies a wind reduction factor (`0.9807 * exp(-0.003 * distance_km)`, developed under the IFRC DREF EAP Cyclone analysis) to the forecast landfall wind speed to estimate expected wind speed at each of the union-level (ADM4) administrative units in the six target districts. This calculation is operationalised in the Shiny monitoring app (`app.R`), which computes distance from the user-supplied landfall point to each union's polygon **boundary** (UTM 46N / EPSG:32646), not its centroid. The team counts model agreements and checks against the 118 km/h threshold at union level to determine whether the readiness, action, or observational trigger criteria are met, then sends a standardised email (plus WhatsApp) to the distribution list including the RC, HCTT members, UN agencies, OCHA and CERF.

## Trigger logic

- **Keys off:** Multi-model wind speed forecast consensus (BMD, IMD, ECMWF, GFS, JTWC) at the union level in six coastal districts, using a wind-reduction-factor model applied to the forecast landfall wind speed; a well-formed low-pressure/depression system in the Bay of Bengal for Stage II; observed wind speed at landfall for Stage III.
- **Decision rule (plain language):** If at least two of the five listed forecast models predict wind speeds of 118 km/h or higher in the coastal pilot districts with 3–5 days of lead time, the readiness trigger fires and CERF disburses the full amount to partners (who begin readiness activities only). If the same two-model consensus holds with up to 72 hours of lead time and a well-formed low pressure or depression has formed in the Bay, the action trigger fires and full implementation begins immediately, no further approval required. If a readiness trigger was missed but the action trigger is reached, disbursement and implementation both start at that point. If neither forecast-based trigger fires but observed wind at landfall reaches 118 km/h (confirmed by BMD/IMD, or by consensus of at least two of ECMWF/GFS/JTWC), the observational trigger fires and 100% of funds can be spent; early-response activities under this trigger can run for up to ~4 weeks after landfall, with geographic reprioritisation if the actually-impacted area differs from the six CERF districts. The doc notes there is a 50% chance the readiness trigger is not reached while the action trigger is (or vice versa) — the two are independent checks, not a strict staged funnel.
- **Activation structure:** Three sequential stages — Stage I (Readiness, 3–5 days), Stage II (Action, ≤72 hours), Stage III (Observational, at/after landfall, early response window up to ~4 weeks). The observational trigger can only fire if the action trigger did not.
- **Edge cases:** activation with <24 hours' lead time is left to agency/RC discretion on whether it is safe to implement (vs. shifting to early response); agencies must otherwise observe government-mandated facility closures starting 12 hours before landfall.
- **Calibration:** The 118 km/h threshold corresponds to a 1-in-4.75-year return period based on historical cyclone data for the Bangladesh coast (1977–2023, per the district-ranking table; the Annex 1 event table spans 1991–2023). At this wind speed, structural damage modelling (BDRCS/GRC survey of ~200 rural households) indicates ~30% (central zone) / ~25% (western zone) household asset damage — generalised to "≥25% of houses damaged" — or ≥40% of union population affected.
- **Authoritative source:** 2025 framework PDF (`framework_doc`). The Shiny app (`app.R`) implements the union-level wind speed calculation for the monitoring team; it does not make a go/no-go decision automatically.
- **Operated by:** OCHA/CHD (monitoring team) and the AA-TWG. The RC, in consultation with MoDMR, can pause an activation in outlier cases where a storm track would clearly cause minimal impact; no external platform (IRI, INSIVUMEH, etc.) runs the trigger.

## Monitoring

The three-person monitoring team (Ahmadul Hassan, Pauline Ndirangu, Leonardo Milano) monitors forecasts twice daily during cyclone seasons (April–June and October–November) using the Shiny app (`app.R`). The app accepts a landfall location and forecast wind speed as input, applies the wind reduction formula to compute expected wind speed at each ADM4 union in the six target districts, and highlights unions above the (user-editable, default 118 km/h) threshold on a Leaflet map and in summary/detail tables (with CSV download). Results are communicated via standardised email and WhatsApp to the distribution list (RC, HCTT members, UN agencies, OCHA, CERF), including an intervention map. The monitoring app is containerised (image `shiny-azure-app-service`) and intended for Azure App Service. The GHA workflow `container-registry.yaml` only **builds and pushes** the image to Azure Container Registry on every push to `master` — there is no deploy step in the repo, so the App Service that pulls the image (and whether it is a prod or dev slot) is configured outside the repo. The Dockerfile and `R/lines.R` carry commented-out DEV blob-SAS build args (`DSCI_AZ_BLOB_DEV_SAS`), residue from development against the dev storage environment; see `discrepancies`.

There is no automated monitoring pipeline — the monitoring team manually inputs forecast wind speeds and landfall location from the five model sources into the app. Trigger confirmation is a human judgment call against the multi-model consensus rule.

## Historical activations

Never activated (as of ingestion date 2026-07-13). The framework has been in endorsed status since April 2025 and was under development/endorsed since December 2023 (with a May 2024 revision). No real cyclone has met the trigger criteria in the target districts during either period. The closest case was Tropical Cyclone Remal (May 2024, under the prior 2023 version): per the framework doc's own narrative the AA activation thresholds were not met early enough for anticipatory action, and CERF instead repurposed the existing AA projects for post-disaster relief — i.e. not a trigger-based activation of this framework.

These are for return-period calibration only — none activated this framework.

## Key decisions & rationale

**118 km/h threshold:** Chosen as the wind speed at which structural damage modelling (BDRCS/GRC household survey, ~200 homes) predicts ~30% asset damage in the central zone and ~25% in the western zone (PDF Table 2; note the table's own per-wall-type cells do not cleanly reconcile to those weighted averages — see `discrepancies`), generalised to "≥25% of houses damaged", and/or ≥40% of union population affected. This also aligns with the national EAP threshold approved by the Government of Bangladesh and the IFRC/BDRCS EAP.

**Multi-model consensus (≥2 of 5):** Reduces false alarms from any single model while preserving responsiveness. ECMWF is noted as showing landfall likelihood, useful for activation decisions; JTWC and IMD provide good skill after cyclonic storm (CS) formation; forecast track error grows quickly beyond 48 hours (Annex 1, Table 3 performance data), so models are treated as usable for readiness but not reliable enough alone for action beyond that horizon.

**Three-stage design:** Readiness at 3–5 days maximises lead time for pre-positioning and beneficiary registration; action at ≤72 hours is when forecast skill is deemed sufficient for activation; the observational trigger provides a backstop (with an early-response window of up to ~4 weeks post-landfall) for cases where the storm strengthens unexpectedly near landfall.

**CERF pre-arrangement:** The RC and ERC pre-approve the CERF allocation ($4M) so that funds are released automatically upon trigger — no case-by-case approval needed, on a no-regrets basis, once endorsed and pre-approved. CERF disburses even if only the readiness trigger fires, but partners hold action-phase funds until the action trigger is reached. CERF funding must be triggered within two years of the ERC's pre-approval (25 April 2025 → drives `valid_until` 2027-04-25).

## Changes from previous version

The 2025 version (endorsed April 2025) supersedes the 2023 version (endorsed December 2023, with a revision endorsed May 2024). Key changes:

1. **Removal of BMD Signal threshold:** The 2023 trigger required BMD Signal 1+ (readiness) and Signal 5+ (action) as explicit AND-conditions alongside the multi-model consensus. The 2025 version removes these entirely. The trigger is now purely multi-model forecast-based. This is a significant operational simplification — the trigger no longer depends on BMD issuing a named warning signal, which could introduce bureaucratic delay.

2. **Addition of Stage III Observational Trigger:** A new post-landfall backstop trigger was added, with an early-response window of up to ~4 weeks after landfall. This activates if the cyclone reaches 118 km/h at landfall without having triggered Stages I or II. This covers the "rapid intensification near coast" scenario.

3. **Return period adjustment:** From 1-in-5 years (2023) to 1-in-4.75 years (2025). The threshold wind speed (118 km/h) is unchanged; the return period figure reflects updated historical analysis.

4. **Edge-case handling expanded:** The 2025 version adds a more nuanced section on sub-24-hour lead time scenarios, permitting agency-level discretion rather than blanket non-activation, alongside a government-mandated 12-hour pre-landfall facility-closure rule.

5. **Funding model change (not simply "expansion"):** The 2023 PDF allocated a **$7.5M CERF envelope shared across both the cyclone and the monsoon-floods frameworks** — the same pool of funds served whichever hazard triggered first, and triggering one would deplete it for the other. The 2025 PDF allocates a **dedicated $4M CERF envelope for cyclones alone**, complemented by co-funding (doc-stated $5,951,987, though the line items printed in the same table sum to $6,015,987 — see discrepancies) for a doc-stated total of $9,951,987. So the cyclone-specific CERF commitment changed from a *shared* $7.5M to a *ring-fenced* $4M, while co-funding grew substantially — adding Start Network ($1.775M), Islamic Relief ($64K), Save the Children ($150K), and larger UNICEF and WFP co-funding. This is a de-pooling plus a co-funding expansion, not a single "budget grew" story.

6. **Monitoring app:** A new Shiny monitoring app (`ds-aa-bgd-cyclone-monitoring`) was developed to operationalise the union-level wind speed calculation, now computing distance to each union's polygon boundary rather than its centroid (see discrepancies). No equivalent tool was documented in the 2023 version.

## Open questions / known issues

- **Budget inconsistency in 2025 PDF:** Executive summary states $8,026,987 total; financing table states $9,951,987, and that table's own line items sum to $10,015,987. None of the three figures agree. Recommend confirming the true pre-arranged total with OCHA/CHD.
- **No analysis repo:** The calibration underlying the 118 km/h / 1-in-4.75-year return period, and the wind-reduction-formula constants themselves, are not reproducible from the repo. If the threshold or formula needs to be updated (e.g., after a future activation and evaluation), there is no code to update — only the PDF narrative and annex graphs.
- **App "in development" label:** The monitoring app UI still shows "This tool is in development" — this should be updated to reflect the endorsed framework status if the app is being used operationally.
- **Companion analysis repo missing:** There is no separate analysis/exploration repo for this framework (unlike some other AA frameworks). All technical derivation exists only in the PDF. If this analysis was done in a separate (private or unpublished) repository, it should be linked here.
- **Threshold editability in app:** The trigger threshold is user-editable in the Shiny app UI. While operationally useful for scenario testing, this creates a risk of accidental non-standard threshold use during a real monitoring event.
- **Observational trigger geography:** The PDF notes that if the observational trigger fires, the target districts may differ from those actually impacted, requiring geographic reprioritisation. The monitoring app does not currently implement any observational wind data input — it is purely forecast-based. How observational trigger monitoring (and the ~4-week early-response window) is operationalised is not documented in the repo.
